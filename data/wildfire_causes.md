# Wildfire Causes and Ignition Sources

## Human-Caused Ignitions
The majority of wildfires in the United States — roughly 85% — are caused by humans. Common causes include:

- **Power lines**: Electrical equipment failures and downed power lines are a leading cause, especially in California. The 2018 Camp Fire, which destroyed the town of Paradise, was caused by a faulty PG&E transmission line.
- **Campfires**: Unattended or improperly extinguished campfires remain one of the most preventable causes of wildfires.
- **Arson**: Deliberate fire-setting accounts for a significant number of annual incidents.
- **Equipment use**: Sparks from machinery, vehicles, or tools used during dry conditions can quickly ignite surrounding vegetation.
- **Debris burning**: Burning yard waste or agricultural debris that escapes containment.
- **Cigarettes**: Discarded cigarettes, especially along roadsides, can ignite dry grass.

## Natural Ignitions
About 15% of wildfires are sparked by natural causes:

- **Lightning**: The most common natural ignition source. Dry lightning storms — where rain evaporates before reaching the ground — are especially dangerous because they deliver electrical strikes without the fire-suppressing moisture.
- **Volcanic activity**: In regions near active volcanoes, lava flows and ejected embers can ignite nearby vegetation.
- **Spontaneous combustion**: Rare, but possible when organic materials like peat or compost generate enough internal heat.

## The Role of Drought
Even a small spark becomes catastrophic under drought conditions. When vegetation loses moisture content — measured as "fuel moisture" — it ignites more easily and burns more intensely. Dead fuel moisture below 10% is considered critically dangerous. The 2020 California wildfire season followed the driest August on record, helping explain why that year saw a record 4.2 million acres burned.

## Ignition vs. Spread
It's important to distinguish between ignition and spread. Many fires are ignited but quickly contained. What turns a small fire into a megafire is a combination of:
- Low humidity
- High temperatures
- Strong winds (especially offshore "Diablo" or "Santa Ana" winds)
- Accumulated fuel loads from decades of fire suppression
