# Wildfire Prevention and Community Safety

## Home Hardening
The single most effective action homeowners in fire-prone areas can take is "home hardening" — making the structure itself more resistant to ignition:

- **Roofing**: Metal, tile, or Class-A asphalt shingles resist ember ignition far better than wood shake
- **Vents**: Ember-resistant vents prevent firebrands from entering attic and crawl spaces
- **Windows**: Double-paned or tempered glass resists radiant heat longer
- **Decks**: Composite or non-combustible decking materials instead of wood
- **Gutters**: Metal gutters with gutter guards prevent accumulation of dead leaves

Research shows that most homes in wildfires ignite from embers landing on or near the structure — not from direct flame contact. This means home hardening can save a house even when the fire front passes nearby.

## Defensible Space
Defensible space is the buffer created between a home and the surrounding vegetation. California law requires:

- **Zone 0 (0–5 ft)**: Non-combustible zone immediately around the home. No mulch, no wood piles, no plants touching the structure.
- **Zone 1 (0–30 ft)**: "Lean, clean, and green." Irrigated, well-spaced vegetation. Remove dead plants and prune branches up to 6–10 feet from the ground.
- **Zone 2 (30–100 ft)**: Reduce fuel density. Space plants so fire cannot easily travel between them. Mow dry grasses.

## Community-Level Strategies

### Prescribed Burns
Controlled, intentional burns during safe weather conditions reduce accumulated fuel loads. Indigenous communities in California, Australia, and elsewhere practiced cultural burning for thousands of years. Modern land managers are increasingly reincorporating prescribed fire after decades of suppression-only policy.

### Forest Thinning
Mechanical removal of excess vegetation — particularly ladder fuels (low shrubs that can carry fire up into tree canopies) — is labor-intensive but effective.

### Community Wildfire Protection Plans (CWPPs)
Formal documents developed collaboratively between communities, local agencies, and the USFS to identify local risks and prioritize mitigation investments.

## Evacuation Planning
Waiting for an official evacuation order can be too late during fast-moving fires:

- **Know your zone**: Most counties use lettered or numbered evacuation zones. Know yours.
- **Go Early**: Voluntarily evacuating at "Evacuation Warning" (rather than "Order") dramatically improves safety outcomes and reduces road congestion.
- **The "Go Bag"**: Pre-pack essentials — medications, documents, chargers, water, clothing — so you can leave in minutes.
- **Two routes**: Know two ways out of your neighborhood in case one is blocked.

## Warning Systems
- **CAL FIRE Watch Duty**: Lookout towers staffed during high-risk periods
- **Wireless Emergency Alerts**: Automatic messages sent to phones in the alert zone
- **AlertWildfire Camera Network**: Over 900 cameras across the Western US provide real-time visual monitoring
