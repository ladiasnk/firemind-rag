# Climate Change and the Wildfire Crisis

## The Changing Baseline
Wildfires have always been part of many ecosystems. What has changed is the scale, frequency, and intensity of fires — driven by a warmer, drier climate baseline.

Key observed changes in the Western United States:
- The wildfire season has extended by 2–3 months compared to the 1970s
- The area burned annually has doubled since the 1980s
- Nine of the ten largest California wildfires on record have occurred since 2017
- "Gigafires" (fires exceeding 1 million acres) are becoming more common

## Mechanisms Linking Climate and Fire

### Higher Temperatures
Warmer temperatures increase evapotranspiration — the process by which plants lose water to the atmosphere. This dries out vegetation even without reduced rainfall, creating fuels that ignite more easily.

### Drought
Multi-year droughts, made more likely by climate change, kill trees and shrubs at landscape scale. The Western US experienced a "megadrought" from 2000–2022 — the driest 22-year period in at least 1,200 years, according to tree-ring research.

### Earlier Snowmelt
In mountain regions, snow is a critical moisture source. When warmer springs cause snowpack to melt earlier, soils and vegetation dry out weeks sooner, extending the window of dangerous fire conditions.

### Vapor Pressure Deficit (VPD)
VPD measures how "thirsty" the atmosphere is — its capacity to pull moisture from plants and soil. As temperatures rise, VPD increases, accelerating fuel drying. Research by Park Williams at UCLA has shown VPD to be one of the strongest predictors of annual burned area.

## Ecosystem Impacts

### Forest Die-offs
Bark beetles, weakened by heat stress, have killed billions of trees across the Western US. Dead standing trees ("snags") are highly flammable and provide enormous fuel loads.

### Shifting Vegetation Zones
As climate zones shift upward in elevation and poleward in latitude, fire-adapted and non-fire-adapted vegetation communities are being reshuffled — sometimes into fire regimes they aren't adapted to survive.

### Post-Fire Landscapes
High-severity fires can prevent forests from regenerating by killing seeds and root systems. Some areas that burned in recent megafires are converting to shrublands or grasslands rather than returning to forest — which burn more frequently and can create self-reinforcing fire cycles.

## The Fire-Climate Feedback Loop
Wildfires release enormous amounts of carbon stored in vegetation and soil, contributing to greenhouse gas emissions. The 2020 California wildfires alone emitted more CO₂ than the state's entire electric power sector. This creates a feedback loop: climate change drives more fire, and more fire accelerates climate change.

## What the Models Say
Climate projections consistently show:
- Further increases in fire weather conditions across the Western US, southern Europe, Australia, and parts of Africa and South America
- More frequent extreme fire weather days (high temperature + low humidity + high wind)
- Increased likelihood of compound events (drought + heat wave + fire weather simultaneously)

IPCC AR6 (2021) identified wildfire as one of the key climate risk areas requiring urgent adaptation planning.
